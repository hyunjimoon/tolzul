## Abstract

Entrepreneurship research and practice share a common assumption: more capital is better. Resources enable experimentation, attract talent, and provide runway for iteration. We test this assumption using panel data on 123,906 technology ventures. Contrary to expectations, we find that early-stage capital is associated with reduced strategic flexibility (Ï = -0.052, p < 0.001), and that flexibility strongly predicts growth (Ï = +0.159, p < 0.001). Ventures that maintained strategic flexibility achieved 2.7Ã— higher growth than those that remained rigid. We propose that capital creates commitment pressure: the promises made to secure funding attract like-minded stakeholders, compress belief variance, and raise the cost of strategic change. This "Golden Cage" mechanism explains why well-resourced ventures sometimes underperform scrappier competitors. The findings establish boundary conditions for resource-based theory and suggest that in high-uncertainty environments, deprivation may paradoxically enable success.

## 1.1 The Resource Advantage Prescription

Resource-based theory holds that superior resources generate superior performance (Barney 1991). Applied to entrepreneurship, this logic suggests that early-stage capital provides multiple advantages: runway to iterate, signals of quality to attract talent, and competitive firepower for market entry. This prescription has become deeply embedded in entrepreneurial practice. Founders celebrate large funding rounds; venture capitalists compete for deals; success stories are narrated through the lens of capital accumulation. The underlying assumption is that resources enable experimentation and learning, and that more experimentation leads to better outcomes.

We formalize this conventional wisdom:

## 1.3 Research Question

We ask: **When does early-stage commitment become a constraint on growth?**

This question addresses a tension in entrepreneurial strategy. Capital provides resources for adaptation, but it also creates expectations for execution. The promises made to secure funding may limit the scope for subsequent change. Understanding when and why this constraint binds is critical for both founders and investors.

---

## 1.4 Theoretical Framework: The Golden Cage

We propose that capital creates commitment pressure through what we term the "Golden Cage" mechanism. The core logic proceeds in three steps:

**Step 1: Capital requires commitment.** To secure funding, founders make specific promisesâ€”about technology, markets, milestones. These promises are the basis on which investors evaluate the opportunity.

**Step 2: Commitment attracts like-minded stakeholders.** Specific promises attract investors, employees, and partners who share the articulated vision. This creates stakeholder homogeneity: everyone agrees on the direction.

**Step 3: Homogeneity blocks adaptation.** When new information suggests the need for strategic change, homogeneous teams struggle to update. The shared commitment that facilitated coordination now prevents learning. The venture is trapped in a "Golden Cage"â€”well-resourced but unable to adapt.

## 1.6 Contributions

This paper makes three contributions.

First, we identify boundary conditions for resource-based theory in entrepreneurship. The resource-advantage view holds when resources can be deployed flexibly. When resource acquisition creates binding commitmentsâ€”as in early-stage venture fundingâ€”the relationship between resources and performance may be attenuated or reversed.

Second, we provide the first empirical quantification of commitment cost. The 2.7Ã— flexibility gap represents the performance difference between ventures that adapted and those that did not. This gives content to abstract notions of "core rigidity" (Leonard-Barton 1992) and "success trap" (Levinthal and March 1993).

Third, we offer actionable guidance for practitioners. For founders: recognize that funding success creates commitment pressure; deliberately preserve belief diversity. For investors: understand that belief homogeneity is a risk factor; consider backing founders who maintain skeptics among their stakeholders.
