## 4.1 Summary of Findings

This paper asked when early-stage commitment becomes a constraint on growth. Using panel data on 123,906 technology ventures, we documented that capital creates commitment pressure that reduces strategic flexibility, and that flexibility strongly predicts growth.

Three findings emerge:

1. **Flexibility drives growth.** Ventures that demonstrated strategic flexibility achieved significantly higher growth (Ï = +0.159, p < 0.001). The most flexible quartile achieved 2.7Ã— higher growth than the most rigid quartile.

## 4.3 Practical Implications

**For founders.** Recognize that funding success creates commitment pressure. The promises that secured capital attract stakeholders who believe in those promises. When circumstances suggest the need for change, homogeneous teams struggle to adapt. Deliberately preserve belief diversity: include skeptics among stakeholders, maintain board members who question the thesis, and resist the urge to fire doubters.

**For investors.** Belief homogeneity is a risk factor, not a sign of alignment. When everyone agrees, no one is positioned to champion alternatives when conditions change. Consider backing founders who maintain internal skeptics. Recognize that "strong thesis alignment" between founders and investors may create exactly the conditions for epistemic lock-in.

**For positioning strategy.** The interaction between capital and positioning (H3) has direct implications. Ventures pursuing the Analyst channel (precise positioning) should be cautious about large early roundsâ€”capital amplifies rigidity. Ventures pursuing the Believer channel (vague positioning) can accept larger roundsâ€”flexibility is preserved through positioning.

## 4.6 Conclusion

Resource-based theory assumes that resources can be deployed flexibly. Our findings suggest this assumption is violated in early-stage venture finance, where resource acquisition creates binding commitments that constrain subsequent adaptation.

The strategic implication is that capital has a dark side. The promises that secure funding attract like-minded stakeholders, compress belief heterogeneity, and raise the cost of strategic change. Well-funded ventures may find themselves in a "Golden Cage"â€”resourced but rigid, capable but constrained.
