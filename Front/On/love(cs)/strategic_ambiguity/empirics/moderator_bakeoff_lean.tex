\hypertarget{executive-summary}{%
\section{Executive Summary}\label{executive-summary}}

We have identified a \textbf{reversal pattern} in how strategic
ambiguity affects startup success:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{H1 (Early Funding)}: Vagueness \textbf{positively} predicts
  Series A funding amounts
\item
  \textbf{H2 (Growth)}: Vagueness \textbf{negatively} predicts Series B+
  progression
\end{itemize}

To understand \textbf{for whom} this pattern holds, we tested two
competing moderators:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Architecture (is\_hardware)}: Integration cost hypothesis --
  vagueness affects modular (software) and integrated (hardware/biotech)
  startups differently
\item
  \textbf{Founder Credibility (is\_serial)}: Reputation hypothesis --
  vagueness affects serial vs.~first-time founders differently
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Key Finding}: The \textbf{is\_hardware} moderator shows
\textbf{clearer interaction patterns} with stronger slope differences
between groups, while \textbf{is\_serial} shows weaker moderation
effects.

\textbf{Question for Advisors}: Which moderator tells a more compelling
story for entrepreneurship research and practice?

\textbf{Which moderator should we prioritize for the main paper?}

\hypertarget{option-a-architecture-is_hardware}{%
\subsubsection{Option A: Architecture
(is\_hardware)}\label{option-a-architecture-is_hardware}}

\textbf{Pros}: - Integration cost is a tangible, measurable construct -
Directly connects to operations management literature - Clear
implications for startup architecture decisions - Stronger visual
interaction pattern (different slopes)

\textbf{Cons}: - May be seen as ``obvious'' (hardware is harder to
pivot) - Less directly tied to founder characteristics - Potential
confounding with capital intensity

\hypertarget{option-b-founder-credibility-is_serial}{%
\subsubsection{Option B: Founder Credibility
(is\_serial)}\label{option-b-founder-credibility-is_serial}}

\textbf{Pros}: - Direct connection to entrepreneurship literature on
serial founders - Policy-relevant (founder development programs) -
Clearer signaling mechanism (reputation substitution) - More
human-centric narrative

\textbf{Cons}: - Weaker statistical interaction (parallel slopes) -
Lower minority class prevalence - Reputation effects may be harder to
manipulate in practice
